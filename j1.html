Your Rights During the USCIS Adjudication Pause

In December 2025 and January 2026, USCIS issued two policy memoranda — PM-602-0192 and PM-602-0194 — implementing a broad pause on final decisions for immigration applications from individuals from approximately 40 countries, including Iran.

Updated as of April 30, 2026 · Immigration policy is changing rapidly. Consult an attorney before making any decisions.

What is happening

For most types of applications — including student visas (F-1), work visas (H-1B), green card applications, and asylum — USCIS is still accepting applications and conducting interviews, but is not issuing final approvals or denials.1

On March 30, 2026, USCIS announced that it would lift its pause on processing applications from “thoroughly screened asylum seekers from non-high-risk countries.” However, USCIS has not defined which countries it considers “high-risk” and has not provided written guidance on this policy change. The pause continues for individuals from travel-ban countries, including Iran.2

Re-review of previously approved benefits

USCIS is conducting a re-review of benefit requests that were approved on or after January 20, 2021, for individuals from designated countries. The date that matters is when USCIS approved your benefits — not when you entered the United States. This re-review authority derives from executive policy memoranda (PM-602-0192), not from a change in immigration statute.

Additionally, USCIS officers are conducting significantly increased interview scrutiny. Interviews are taking longer and officers are asking detailed questions about intent and background. This is separate from the pause — it means adjudication may take significantly longer even when it does resume.

How did this happen

These measures built on a previously undisclosed USCIS memorandum from November 2025 — which remained secret until reporting by the New York Times — that directed adjudicators to consider country-specific facts and circumstances as negative discretionary factors when processing immigration benefits. This policy effectively allows adjudicators to weigh an applicant’s country of origin against them, laying the groundwork for the country-based approach that defines the current pause.13

Who is affected

USCIS has clarified that it uses country of origin — not current citizenship — to determine whether an applicant falls under the pause. If your country of origin is Iran or another travel-ban country, holding dual nationality with a non-designated country does not exempt you. For example, Canadian citizens filing for TN visas face the same pause if their country of origin is a designated country.13

  • All asylum seekers are affected by the pause regardless of nationality.
  • For nationals of designated countries — including Iran — all USCIS-administered immigration benefits are paused. All cases previously approved on or after January 20, 2021 are also subject to re-review and potential re-interview.
  • All USCIS humanitarian benefits for nationals of travel-ban countries are also paused without exception, including U visas, VAWA self-petitions, I-751 waivers of the joint filing requirement based on abuse, and humanitarian parole.

Travelling while your application is pending

Whether you can travel while an application is pending is a case-by-case assessment that varies significantly based on your immigration status and individual risk factors. Travel is especially risky in the current environment because the State Department is actively exercising its power to revoke visas.13

There are two types of visa revocations:

  • Prudential revocation — takes effect only after you leave the United States. Your current status is not affected while you remain here, but you may not be able to re-enter.
  • Immediate revocation — your visa is revoked while you are still in the country. Your visa stamp (the foil in your passport) is separate from your immigration status (which is determined by your I-20, DS-2019, H-1B approval notice, or similar document). However, under certain provisions, an immediate revocation may subject you to deportation proceedings even if you still hold valid underlying status.

Cases that are statutorily ineligible — for example, a naturalization application filed without the requisite period of continuous residence, or any filing that fails to meet a mandatory legal requirement — will be denied by USCIS even during the pause.13

What you should do

Do not file for B-2 tourist status

Some immigration attorneys advise NOT filing for B-2 (tourist visa) status as a fallback strategy. Under the current administration, B-2 status is not a safe backup for individuals on other visa types. Filing for a B-2 extension or change of status can trigger heightened scrutiny of your entire immigration file and may actually accelerate removal proceedings rather than buy you time. Consult an attorney before considering any status change.

Mandamus litigation — when silence becomes actionable

If your case has been pending an unreasonably long time, you may have the option to file a mandamus lawsuit in federal court. Mandamus is a legal action authorized under the Administrative Procedure Act that compels USCIS to make a decision on your case — it does not guarantee approval or denial in your favor.12

Mandamus litigation risks

Mandamus compels USCIS to make a decision — it does not guarantee approval. By forcing your case to be adjudicated, there is a risk that it may be denied sooner than it otherwise would have been. Discuss the risks and benefits thoroughly with an immigration attorney before pursuing mandamus litigation.

Litigation has emerged as a viable option during the pause. Federal court review provides an independent, impartial assessment by a judge — distinct from the executive branch agencies that operate under administration directives. Among the many pause-related lawsuits filed to date, outcomes have been strongly favorable for applicants. However, a mandamus compels USCIS to make a decision — it does not compel approval — so you should be very careful in assessing whether you are a good candidate to pursue litigation.13

If you are considering litigation, work with an attorney who is very familiar with Iranian cases and who can assess the specific risk factors in your case. These factors include whether you worked in a sanctioned industry in Iran, your field of study, your academic and professional connections, family members in Iran and their occupations, and any military service history. The government is conducting heightened scrutiny that requires what attorneys are calling “extreme consistency” — every interaction you have had with the immigration system, from your first application to the present, must be fully consistent. Even slight discrepancies between past filings can be flagged as potential fraud.13

This scrutiny extends to extensive FBI background checks that encompass all interactions with law enforcement or any government agency throughout your life, both inside and outside the United States. Immigration attorneys advise that you should generally not speak with the FBI or other federal investigators about your case or personal history without an attorney present, even if you believe you have nothing to hide.13

Keep your documents ready

More than ever, it is important to keep original evidence of your immigration status on your person at all times. Examples may include your green card, approval notices for your current status, receipt notices for any pending extensions, your passport with visa stamp, your I-20 if you are a student, and your EAD. Workers should also consider carrying recent pay statements, an offer letter, and a recently signed verification-of-employment letter. Students should carry their transcript and other academic documents. For all important documents — birth certificates, immigration filings, financial records — keep originals in a secure location and make sure someone you trust knows where to find them. Consider providing copies to a trusted person as well.13

Memorize the phone number of an immigration attorney you have worked with, and share that number with someone you trust so they know who to call if something happens to you.13

Prepare for emergencies

Prepare for the possibility that you could be suddenly detained with no access to the outside world for at least a short period. An emergency preparedness plan should cover your emergency contacts, how rent and bills will be paid, care for children or pets, and access to your finances. Get to know an immigration lawyer before any problems arise — having an established relationship with an attorney can be critical in an emergency.

Before communicating with your employer or school about your immigration situation, consider speaking with an immigration attorney first. An attorney represents your interests, whereas your employer or school will typically prioritize their own compliance obligations and institutional interests.
