IRANIAN AMERICAN LEGAL DEFENSE FUND Resource Center — Master Document Last Updated: May 8, 2026 • Version 21 [Heading1] Section 1: Know Your Rights Not legal advice. This section is for informational purposes only and does not create an attorney-client relationship. Laws and policies change frequently. Consult a qualified immigration attorney or civil rights lawyer about your specific situation. As an Iranian American, understanding your legal rights is one of the most important things you can do to protect yourself and your family. Whether you are a U.S. citizen, a permanent resident, or here on a visa, certain fundamental protections apply to you under the Constitution and federal law. This section walks you through those rights in practical, everyday terms. [Heading2] 1.1 Your Rights During Police and ICE Encounters If you are stopped by law enforcement or by Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE), the most important thing to understand is that you have rights — and using those rights does not mean you have done anything wrong. Choosing to stay silent or to refuse a search cannot be used against you in court as evidence that you are guilty of something. The Constitution protects you from this. The Fourth Amendment protects you from unreasonable searches and seizures, and the Fifth Amendment protects you from being forced to say things that could be used to incriminate yourself.1 There is an important distinction between the types of warrants officers may present. A judicial warrant is signed by a judge and carries the full authority of the court. An administrative warrant — such as Form I-200, which is issued and signed by an ICE supervisor — does not carry the same legal weight. You are not legally required to open your door for an administrative warrant.2 [Heading3] What You Can Do: Remain calm and do not physically resist, even if you believe the stop is unlawful. Resisting can escalate the situation and may result in additional charges. If an officer asks to search you or your belongings, you may say clearly: “I do not consent to a search.” Even if officers proceed with the search, your verbal objection preserves your legal rights and may be important in court later.3 Ask: “Am I free to go?” If the officer says yes, walk away calmly. If the answer is no, you are being detained, and you should ask for an attorney. If you are not free to go, say: “I want to speak to a lawyer.” Say this clearly and, if necessary, repeat it. Once you have requested a lawyer, you are not required to answer further questions. [Heading3] What You Are Required to Provide — and to Whom: If you are stopped by a local police officer, you are generally required to provide your name and, in some states, to show a state-issued ID if you have one. You are not required to answer questions about your immigration status, country of birth, or how you entered the United States. Local police in most jurisdictions do not have immigration enforcement authority.4 If you are stopped by an ICE or CBP (Customs and Border Protection) agent, you must provide your name and show immigration documents if you have them (such as a green card, visa, or EAD). However, you may refuse to answer substantive questions — such as where you were born, how you entered the country, or your immigration history — by invoking your right to remain silent.5 In either situation, do not lie or present false documents. Making false statements to a federal agent is a federal crime, punishable by up to five years in prison.6 If you are arrested, officers must inform you of your Miranda rights before conducting a custodial interrogation. Miranda rights are the warnings police are required to give before questioning you while you are in custody: that you have the right to remain silent, that anything you say can be used against you in court, that you have the right to a lawyer, and that if you cannot afford a lawyer one will be provided in a criminal case. Statements made before these warnings are given may be inadmissible in court, though limited exceptions exist.7 [Heading3] Understanding the Difference: Stopped, Questioned, Detained, and Arrested These terms have specific legal meanings, and the rights you have depend on which situation you are in: Situation Your Rights Briefly stopped for questioning An officer approaches you and asks questions in a brief encounter — often on the street, in a car, or at your workplace. You must provide your name if asked. You may ask “Am I free to go?” If yes, leave calmly. You do not have to answer questions about your immigration status (unless the officer is ICE or CBP — see above). Stopped and questioned by an immigration officer (ICE or CBP) An ICE or CBP agent approaches you, often at a checkpoint, workplace raid, or your home, and identifies themselves as immigration officers. You must provide your name and immigration documents if you have them. You may decline to answer substantive questions about your immigration history. Say: “I am exercising my right to remain silent. I would like to speak to a lawyer.” Detained (not free to leave, but not formally arrested) If you ask “Am I being detained?” and the officer says yes — or tells you that you are not free to leave — you are detained. Detention is temporary and investigative. You have the right to remain silent. You may ask: “Why am I being detained?” You do not have a right to a government-appointed attorney during a brief investigative detention, but you may request to contact one. Do not sign anything without legal advice. Arrested (formally placed in custody) An officer tells you that you are under arrest, places you in handcuffs, or reads you your rights. You are taken into custody. You have the right to remain silent. You have the right to know the charges against you. You have the right to an attorney — in criminal proceedings, one will be appointed if you cannot afford one. In immigration proceedings, you must obtain your own attorney. Say: “I want to speak to a lawyer” and remain silent. Important distinction between detention and arrest: Detention is temporary — officers hold you briefly while they investigate. During a brief investigative detention, you do not have the right to a government-appointed attorney, though you may request to contact one. Arrest means you are formally in custody and will be processed through the criminal or immigration system. In criminal cases, you have the right to a court-appointed attorney if you cannot afford one. In immigration cases, you have the right to an attorney, but the government will not appoint one for you — you must find and pay for your own, or seek free legal aid.8 If you are placed in immigration proceedings, you can ask the immigration judge or the officer holding you for a List of Pro Bono Legal Service Providers. The Department of Justice maintains this list of free or low-cost organizations that may be able to represent you, and you have the right to receive a copy. [Heading3] Your Rights Regarding Searches: During a routine stop, an officer may conduct a limited pat-down of your outer clothing if the officer has reasonable suspicion that you may be armed. This does not require your consent. However, a more thorough search of your person — such as going through your pockets — generally requires either your consent, a warrant, or a lawful arrest.9 Your belongings (bags, backpack, vehicle) may be searched if you give consent, if officers have a warrant, or in certain circumstances such as a search incident to arrest. If you do not consent, say clearly: “I do not consent to a search of my belongings.” Even if officers proceed, your objection is legally preserved. Searches at the border are different. At airports, ports of entry, and other border locations, Customs and Border Protection officers may search your belongings, including phones and laptops, with broader authority than police elsewhere. Your verbal objection is still recorded for legal purposes, and CBP cannot deny a U.S. citizen entry into the country, but they may delay you while they conduct a search. See Section 1.4 for more on airport and border encounters. [Heading3] What You Should Know About ICE Detention: You have the right to remain silent — use it. Anything you say can and will be used against you. You have the right to be represented by an attorney. However, in immigration proceedings, the government will not appoint one for you. You must hire your own attorney or seek assistance from a nonprofit legal aid organization. IALDF maintains a list of resources that may help.10 You have the right to know why you are being held. The officers must inform you of the basis for your detention. You have the right to a phone call. Ask: “May I make a phone call?” Use it to contact a lawyer, family member, or trusted friend. If you are arrested on criminal charges, you have the right to a probable cause determination hearing within 72 hours of your arrest.11 [Heading3] Key Phrases You Can Use: Situation What to Say If you are stopped by police or ICE “I do not consent to a search.” If arrested: “I want to speak to a lawyer.” Then remain silent. If you are asked for documents To police: Provide your name and state ID. You do not have to answer immigration questions. To ICE/CBP: Provide your name and immigration documents if you have them. Then: “I am exercising my right to remain silent.” If you are asked questions about your immigration history “I am exercising my right to remain silent. I would like to speak to a lawyer.” [FARSI TRANSLATION: These key phrases should be translated to Farsi. Include a downloadable/printable phrase card.] [Heading2] 1.2 Your Rights at Home Your home receives the strongest protection under the Fourth Amendment. As a general rule, law enforcement needs a warrant signed by a judge to enter your home.12 [Heading3] What You Can Do: You do not have to open the door. Ask: “Do you have a warrant?” If they say yes, ask to see it slid under the door or held up to a window before opening the door. If there is no judicial warrant (i.e., no judge’s signature), you may say: “I do not consent to entry. Please leave.” An ICE administrative warrant (Form I-200) does not compel you to open your door. Even with a valid judicial warrant, you may ask to see it and ask: “What is this warrant for?” Cooperate, but do not volunteer additional information. If officers claim an emergency, they may enter without a warrant under the “exigent circumstances” exception — for example, if they are pursuing someone they have reason to believe just committed a serious crime, or if someone inside is in immediate danger. You can still ask questions, but in genuine life-safety situations, the law allows officers to enter even without your permission. [Heading2] 1.3 If You Are Stopped, Questioned, or Detained Being stopped, questioned, or detained can be extremely stressful. The most important thing you can do is stay calm, exercise your rights clearly and verbally, and contact an attorney as soon as possible. [Heading2] 1.4 Your Rights at the Airport and Borders Airports and border crossings are legally distinct from other locations. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) officers have broader authority to stop, question, and search travelers at the border than police have during a routine street encounter. That said, you still have rights. [Heading3] When Entering the United States: If you are a U.S. citizen (including naturalized citizens), you have the right to enter the United States. You must present a valid passport or other acceptable travel document. Declining to answer questions may cause delay but cannot ultimately prevent a citizen from entering. [Heading3] Key Phrases for Airport and Border Encounters: Situation What to Say CBP officer asks questions beyond your name and travel purpose “I prefer to answer further questions with an attorney present.” Then wait for a response. CBP officer asks you to consent to a search of your belongings “I do not consent to a search.” Note: CBP may search your belongings anyway at the border, but your objection is noted for legal purposes. You are pulled aside for secondary inspection or prolonged questioning “Am I being detained or am I free to go?” Then: “I would like to contact an attorney.” [Heading2] 1.5 Your Legal Protections Against Discrimination Federal and state laws protect you from discrimination based on your race, national origin, religion, immigration status, and other characteristics. These protections apply broadly — in your workplace, your housing, your school, and in public places. Employment: Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 prohibits employers from refusing to hire, firing, or mistreating you because of your race, national origin, or religion.13 Housing: The Fair Housing Act prohibits discrimination in rental, sales, lending, and other housing-related services based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, disability, and family status.14 Public Accommodations: Title II of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 protects your right to equal service in hotels, restaurants, stores, and public transportation.15 Education: Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 prohibits discrimination in schools and programs receiving federal funds.16 [Heading3] If You Experience Discrimination: Keep a detailed record of any evidence — emails, texts, photos, dates, times, and witness names. Report employment discrimination to the EEOC, housing discrimination to HUD, and education discrimination to the Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights. Consult with an attorney. Many lawyers offer free initial consultations. [Heading2] 1.6 Special Considerations for Iranian Americans and Dual Nationals If you are an Iranian American or a dual national (holding both Iranian and U.S. citizenship), there are additional considerations that affect your rights and safety. [Heading3] Consular Access and Notification: If you are detained or arrested, you may be asked whether you wish to have your country's consulate notified. Under the Vienna Convention on Consular Relations, U.S. authorities are generally required to inform you of this right.17 Important — Consular Notification Risks Before requesting that the Iranian consulate (or the Iranian Interest Section) be notified, carefully consider the potential consequences. Iran has a documented history of monitoring its diaspora communities and nationals abroad. Requesting consular notification creates an official record that the Iranian government could access. While the specific risk to family members in Iran is difficult to quantify, the possibility exists and should be discussed with your attorney before you make this decision. If you are uncertain, tell the officers: “I need to speak with my attorney before making a decision about consular notification.” [Heading3] Military Service Obligations: Iran requires military service for male citizens. Males are generally subject to service obligations until approximately age 49–50, though specific rules and exceptions apply under Iranian law.18 If you are an Iranian national or dual national who has not completed military service, returning to Iran could result in your detention at the border. You may be prevented from leaving Iran until your obligation is fulfilled or a financial penalty is paid. Deferments are available for students who are enrolled full-time in an accredited university. A letter from your university may serve as documentation of this deferment within Iran. However, this is a temporary deferment — once you graduate or withdraw, the service obligation resumes.19 Consult with an immigration attorney before planning any travel to Iran. [Heading3] Travel to Iran — Security Advisory: The U.S. State Department has issued a Level 4 “Do Not Travel” advisory for Iran, citing the risk of arbitrary arrest and detention of dual nationals and the limited ability of the U.S. government to provide consular assistance if you are detained.20 [Heading2] 1.7 How Sanctions Affect Iranian Americans The U.S. government imposes economic sanctions on Iran through the Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC). These sanctions restrict most financial transactions involving Iran and are administered under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA).21 [Heading3] What You Should Know: Most financial transactions with Iran are restricted. However, OFAC authorizes certain activities under “general licenses,” which means you do not need to apply for individual permission. For example, personal remittances to family in Iran for basic living expenses are generally permitted under a general license, and many Iranian Americans routinely send money to family in Iran through authorized channels.22 You cannot import goods from Iran or export goods to Iran without a specific license from OFAC. This includes carpets, art, antiques, and other items. Travel to Iran by U.S. citizens and permanent residents is not categorically prohibited by OFAC. Many Iranian Americans do travel to Iran for family visits. However, certain travel-related transactions may require authorization depending on the purpose of travel. Consult with a sanctions attorney if you are uncertain whether your specific travel plans comply with current regulations.23 Foreign account reporting is not Iran-specific If you have any foreign financial accounts — in Iran or anywhere else in the world — with an aggregate value over $10,000 at any point during the year, you are required to file a Report of Foreign Bank and Financial Accounts (FBAR / FinCEN Form 114). This rule applies to every foreign country, not only Iran, so it is not an extra burden placed on Iranian Americans. What is Iran-specific is OFAC: certain property interests in Iran may be subject to OFAC reporting or blocking requirements separate from FBAR. If you have accounts or property abroad, talk to a tax attorney or CPA familiar with international reporting obligations. [Heading3] If You Are Uncertain: Consult with an attorney who specializes in sanctions and export control law. Check OFAC's website (treasury.gov/ofac) for current regulations and FAQs. Maintain documentation of all transactions involving Iran. This section is for informational purposes only and does not create an attorney-client relationship. Laws and policies change frequently. Immigration policy is changing rapidly during the adjudication pause; consult an immigration attorney before making any decisions. [Heading2] What Is Happening In December 2025 and January 2026, USCIS issued two policy memoranda — PM-602-0192 (December 2, 2025) and PM-602-0194 (January 1, 2026) — implementing a broad pause on final decisions for immigration applications from individuals from approximately 40 countries, including Iran.1 This means that for most types of applications — including student visas (F-1), work visas (H-1B), green card applications, and asylum — USCIS is still accepting applications and conducting interviews, but is not issuing final approvals or denials. On March 30, 2026, USCIS announced that it would lift its pause on processing applications from “thoroughly screened asylum seekers from non-high-risk countries.” However, USCIS has not defined which countries it considers “high-risk” and has not provided written guidance on this policy change. The pause continues for individuals from travel ban countries, including Iran.2 Important — USCIS Re-Review of Previously Approved Benefits USCIS is conducting a re-review of benefit requests that were approved on or after January 20, 2021, for individuals from designated countries. The date that matters is when USCIS approved your benefits — not when you entered the United States. This re-review authority derives from executive policy memoranda (PM-602-0192), not from a change in immigration statute. Additionally, USCIS officers are conducting significantly increased interview scrutiny. Interviews are taking longer and officers are asking detailed questions about intent and background. This is separate from the pause — it means adjudication may take significantly longer even when it does resume. How Did This Happen These measures built on a previously undisclosed USCIS memorandum from November 2025 — which remained secret until reporting by the New York Times — that directed adjudicators to consider country-specific facts and circumstances as negative discretionary factors when processing immigration benefits. This policy effectively allows adjudicators to weigh an applicant's country of origin against them, laying the groundwork for the country-based approach that defines the current pause.13 Who Is Affected USCIS has clarified that it uses country of origin — not current citizenship — to determine whether an applicant falls under the pause. If your country of origin is Iran or another travel-ban country, holding dual nationality with a non-designated country does not exempt you. For example, Canadian citizens filing for TN visas face the same pause if their country of origin is a designated country.13 All asylum seekers are affected by the pause regardless of nationality.13 Separately, for nationals of designated countries — including Iran — all USCIS-administered immigration benefits are paused. All cases previously approved on or after January 20, 2021 are also subject to re-review and potential re-interview.13 All USCIS humanitarian benefits for nationals of travel-ban countries are also paused without exception, including U visas, VAWA self-petitions, I-751 waivers of the joint filing requirement based on abuse, and humanitarian parole.13 Travelling While Your Application is Pending Whether you can travel while an application is pending is a case-by-case assessment that varies significantly based on your immigration status and individual risk factors. Travel is especially risky in the current environment because the State Department is actively exercising its power to revoke visas.13 There are two types of visa revocations: Prudential revocation — takes effect only after you leave the United States. Your current status is not affected while you remain here, but you may not be able to re-enter.13 Immediate revocation — your visa is revoked while you are still in the country. Your visa stamp (the foil in your passport) is separate from your immigration status (which is determined by your I-20, DS-2019, H-1B approval notice, or similar document). However, under certain provisions, an immediate revocation may subject you to deportation proceedings even if you still hold valid underlying status.13 Cases that are statutorily ineligible — for example, a naturalization application filed without the requisite period of continuous residence, or any filing that fails to meet a mandatory legal requirement — will be denied by USCIS even during the pause.13 The sections below are organized by immigration type. Select the category that applies to your situation. [Heading2] OPT and STEM OPT (Optional Practical Training) [Heading3] How the Pause Affects OPT: OPT applications (Form I-765) are subject to the adjudication pause. You can still file, and USCIS will accept your application, but final decisions are not being issued. [Heading3] Automatic Extension Rules: The rules for automatic work permit extensions changed on October 30, 2025, when DHS issued a new interim final rule ending the automatic extension program for most categories.3 Filing Date Automatic Extension Status Before October 30, 2025 Eligible for up to 540-day automatic extension. You can continue working with proof of your pending application. On or after October 30, 2025 Most applicants no longer receive automatic extension. You cannot work without a final USCIS decision. Initial OPT (not a renewal) has never qualified for the 540-day automatic extension. If your initial OPT application is pending, you cannot begin working until USCIS issues your EAD. [Heading3] STEM OPT Extensions: If you timely filed your 24-month STEM OPT extension, you may be eligible for a 180-day automatic extension of your current work authorization. However, your authorization expires at the end of 180 days if USCIS has not issued a decision. Due to the pause, many students are at risk of reaching this limit.4 The automatic 180-day extension of work authorization for timely-filed STEM OPT extensions continues to operate normally and is not affected by the adjudication pause.13 [Heading3] Court Developments: On April 17, 2026, a federal court in Behdin v. Edlow, No. 5:26-cv-00566 (N.D. Cal.), issued a preliminary injunction requiring USCIS to adjudicate certain Form I-765 applications. This ruling is limited to the 32 named plaintiffs in that case and does not automatically extend to other applicants. However, it may provide legal grounds for similarly situated individuals to pursue their own litigation.5 [Heading3] What You Should Do: [ListParagraph] Communicate with your Designated School Official (DSO) immediately about your situation. [ListParagraph] Keep all documentation: USCIS receipts, I-20 form, proof of enrollment, correspondence. [ListParagraph] Know your critical deadlines: I-20 expiration, OPT expiration, STEM OPT 180-day limit. [ListParagraph] Explore options: SEVIS transfer, CPT placement, or other authorization. [ListParagraph] If your status is at risk, consult an immigration attorney about mandamus litigation (see below). [Heading2] F-1 Student Status Your F-1 student status continues as long as you maintain a full course of study and comply with your school's requirements. A delay in an OPT application does not by itself terminate your F-1 status. You can still transfer your SEVIS record from one school to another. The pause primarily affects final decisions on benefit requests, not the SEVIS management system itself.7 This includes transfers for Day 1 CPT after OPT ends, which involve a SEVIS record transfer rather than a USCIS benefit application.13 F-1 students are currently admitted for "duration of status," which means your authorized period of stay is tied to your academic program rather than a fixed calendar date. You do not need to file a status extension as long as you remain enrolled in your program. However, a new rule is expected (not yet effective as of April 2026) that would assign F-1 holders a specific expiration date instead of duration of status.13 If you are currently enrolled and a Curricular Practical Training (CPT) option is available through your program, you can activate it. However, be aware that completing 12 months or more of full-time CPT disqualifies you from Optional Practical Training (OPT). Discuss this trade-off with your DSO before enrolling in CPT.14 Be aware of the 60-day grace period — the time you can remain in the U.S. after your program ends without other valid status. If your program is ending, act before this grace period runs out.8 [Heading2] J-1 Exchange Visitor Status J-1 status is governed by the dates on your DS-2019 form and your program sponsor's approval. The pause affects some J-1 applications, but your underlying status is tied to your sponsor. Contact your program sponsor directly for guidance. You cannot transfer a J-1 program to a different program category — transfers must remain within the same category. If you are considering a program change that involves a different J-1 category, this would require a new application and would be subject to the pause.13 [Heading2] H-1B Work Visa [Heading3] The 240-Day Extension Rule: If your H-1B extension is pending and you filed a timely extension request before your current H-1B expired, you may continue working for your current employer for up to 240 days past your expiration date. This only applies if you filed timely — if your H-1B has already expired without a pending extension, this rule does not protect you.9 H-1B holders specifically benefit from portability, which allows you to transfer to a new employer without being affected by the pause. H-1B portability operates under a separate statutory framework that does not require the pending petition to be adjudicated before you begin work for the new employer. This provision does not apply to other visa classifications such as O-1 or L-1.13 Applications to change from one immigration status to another — for example, from F-1 to H-1B — are not being adjudicated during the pause. If you are currently maintaining valid status in one category, consider preserving that status rather than applying for a change that will not be processed.13 [Heading3] EAD Categories and Auto-Extension: The same October 30, 2025 rule change affects EAD holders in employment-based categories. Check your I-797C notice for your eligibility category:10 [ListParagraph] A03 = Refugees; A05 = Asylees; A10 = Withholding of Removal [ListParagraph] C08 = Pending Asylum; C09 = Pending Adjustment of Status [ListParagraph] C26 = H-4 Dependent Spouses; C31 = VAWA Self-Petitioners Categories that were previously eligible for auto-extension lost that protection on October 30, 2025 for renewals filed on or after that date. Current exceptions: TPS beneficiaries (A12/C19) if announced via Federal Register, STEM OPT recipients (180-day extension), and spouses of L and E visa holders. [Heading2] Employment-Based Green Card (PERM, I-140, I-485) [Heading3] AC21 Portability: If your employer filed an I-140 petition and it has been approved, and you have a pending I-485 that has been pending for at least 180 days, you may be able to change employers without losing your place in line for the green card. This is called “portability” under AC21 (American Competitiveness in the Twenty-First Century Act).11 This gives you flexibility during the pause — you are not locked to the employer that sponsored you. [Heading2] What You Should Do Critical: Do NOT File for B-2 Tourist Status Some immigration attorneys advise NOT filing for B-2 (tourist visa) status as a fallback strategy. Under the current administration, B-2 status is not a safe backup for individuals on other visa types. Filing for a B-2 extension or change of status can trigger heightened scrutiny of your entire immigration file and may actually accelerate removal proceedings rather than buy you time. Consult an attorney before considering any status change. [Heading3] Mandamus Litigation — When Silence Becomes Actionable If your case has been pending an unreasonably long time, you may have the option to file a mandamus lawsuit in federal court. Mandamus is a legal action authorized under the Administrative Procedure Act that compels USCIS to make a decision on your case — it does not guarantee approval or denial in your favor.12 Disclaimer — Mandamus Litigation Risks Mandamus compels USCIS to make a decision — it does not guarantee approval. By forcing your case to be adjudicated, there is a risk that it may be denied sooner than it otherwise would have been. Discuss the risks and benefits thoroughly with an immigration attorney before pursuing mandamus litigation. Only pursue mandamus after careful consultation with counsel. Do not assume that a pending case automatically protects you from all enforcement actions. Continue to comply with all requirements of your current status. Litigation has emerged as a viable option during the pause. Federal court review provides an independent, impartial assessment by a judge — distinct from the executive branch agencies that operate under administration directives. Among the many pause-related lawsuits filed to date, outcomes have been strongly favorable for applicants. However, a mandamus compels USCIS to make a decision — it does not compel approval — so you should be very careful in assessing whether you are a good candidate to pursue litigation.13 If you are considering litigation, work with an attorney who is very familiar with Iranian cases and who can assess the specific risk factors in your case. These factors include whether you worked in a sanctioned industry in Iran, your field of study, your academic and professional connections, family members in Iran and their occupations, and any military service history. The government is conducting heightened scrutiny that requires what attorneys are calling "extreme consistency" — every interaction you have had with the immigration system, from your first application to the present, must be fully consistent. Even slight discrepancies between past filings can be flagged as potential fraud.13 This scrutiny extends to extensive FBI background checks that encompass all interactions with law enforcement or any government agency throughout your life, both inside and outside the United States. Immigration attorneys advise that you should generally not speak with the FBI or other federal investigators about your case or personal history without an attorney present, even if you believe you have nothing to hide.13 Keep Your Documents Ready More than ever, it is important to keep original evidence of your immigration status on your person at all times. Depending on your situation, examples may include your green card, approval notices for your current status, receipt notices for any pending extensions, your passport with visa stamp, your I-20 if you are a student, and your EAD. Workers should also consider carrying recent pay statements, an offer letter, and a recently signed verification of employment letter. Students should carry their transcript and other academic documents. For all important documents — birth certificates, immigration filings, financial records — keep originals in a secure location and make sure someone you trust knows where to find them. A: In December 2025 and January 2026, the Executive Branch issued two policy memoranda (PM-602-0192 and PM-602-0194) directing USCIS to pause adjudication of most immigration benefit applications for nationals of approximately 40 countries, including Iran. USCIS is not currently making decisions on pending cases from these countries. Q: Does IALDF provide legal representation? A: No. IALDF does not provide legal representation or legal services. We provide resources, referrals, and advocacy support. If you need legal help, you will need to hire an attorney or find pro bono services. Q: I'm a U.S. citizen. Does the travel ban or pause affect me? Q: I hold dual nationality (Iranian and another foreign country). Does that exempt me from the pause? A: No. USCIS uses country of origin to determine whether you fall under the travel-ban pause. Holding citizenship in a non-designated country does not exempt you if your country of origin is Iran or another designated country.9 Q: What can I do to be prepared in the unfortunate event of a sudden arrest? A: Create an emergency preparedness plan. Keep original immigration documents on your person. Memorize the phone number of an immigration attorney. Prepare a plan that covers child care, finances, pet care, and emergency contacts in case you are suddenly unavailable. Share the plan with someone you trust. See the IALDF Family Preparedness Plan for a detailed template.9 A: U.S. citizens cannot be denied entry to the United States because of national origin. The USCIS adjudication pause does not apply to applications you might have already-naturalized citizens are not subject to it. However, family-based petitions you file (such as an I-130 for an Iranian relative abroad) can be affected, because the beneficiary is from a designated country. [Heading2] Travel & Visas Q: Can I travel to Iran as a U.S. citizen or green card holder? A: There is no U.S. legal prohibition on a citizen or permanent resident traveling to Iran, but the State Department has issued a Level 4 “Do Not Travel” advisory citing the risk of arbitrary arrest and detention of dual nationals. Iran does not recognize dual citizenship — if you enter Iran, the Iranian government may treat you exclusively as an Iranian citizen, including with respect to military service obligations. Talk to an immigration attorney before booking travel. Q: Can I leave the U.S. and re-enter while my application is pending? A: Generally, no — leaving the U.S. while you have a pending I-485, asylum application, or some other benefit applications can be treated as abandonment unless you have an approved Advance Parole document (Form I-131). For F-1 and H-1B holders, separate travel rules apply. Always consult an attorney before international travel during the pause. Q: Can I travel internationally while the pause is in effect? A: This is a case-by-case assessment that depends heavily on your immigration status and individual risk factors. Travel is currently riskier than usual because the State Department is actively revoking visas. There are two types of revocations: prudential (takes effect after you leave the U.S.) and immediate (your visa is revoked while you are here, which may subject you to deportation even with valid underlying status). Consult an immigration attorney before any international travel.9 Q: My visa expired but my extension is pending — am I out of status? A: Not necessarily. If you filed your extension before your status expired, you are generally considered to be in a period of authorized stay while it is pending, even if your I-94 has expired. For H-1B holders, the 240-day rule allows you to keep working for the same employer for up to 240 days past the I-94 expiration date. Talk to your employer's immigration counsel. Q: What should I tell CBP if asked about my Iranian citizenship? A: Be truthful — making false statements to a federal officer is a federal crime. If you are a dual national, you can disclose that. If asked questions beyond your name and travel purpose that you are uncomfortable answering, you can say: “I prefer to answer further questions with an attorney present.” CBP cannot deny entry to a U.S. citizen but may delay you. For green card holders, refusing to answer can result in extended secondary inspection, but the government cannot revoke your status at the airport — only an immigration judge can do that. [Heading2] Money & Sanctions Q: Can I send money to family in Iran? A: Yes, in most cases, through authorized channels. OFAC's general license at 31 C.F.R. § 560.545 authorizes non-commercial personal remittances to family members in Iran for basic living expenses, sent through a U.S. depository institution or U.S.-registered money transmitter. Some U.S. banks may decline to process Iran-related transactions even when they are legal. Keep documentation. Q: Will my U.S. bank close my account because I'm Iranian? A: U.S. banks cannot lawfully close an account solely because of national origin — that is prohibited national-origin discrimination. However, banks have wide discretion to manage perceived sanctions or fraud risk and may close accounts citing those reasons. If a bank closes your account, ask in writing for the specific reason. If you believe national origin played a role, you can file a complaint with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (consumerfinance.gov) and consult a civil rights attorney. Q: Do I have to report my Iranian bank account on FBAR? A: Yes — but this is not Iran-specific. FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) applies to any U.S. person with foreign financial accounts whose aggregate value exceeded $10,000 at any point during the year, in any country. The threshold and rule are the same whether the account is in Canada, the U.K., or Iran. Talk to a CPA or tax attorney with international reporting experience. Q: Can I inherit property in Iran? A: U.S. sanctions law generally permits receipt of personal inheritance from family in Iran, but the transfer of funds or property may be subject to OFAC reporting or licensing requirements depending on the value, type of property, and timing. A sanctions attorney should review the specific facts before any transfer. [Heading2] Asylum & Iran-Specific Risk Q: I served mandatory military service in Iran — am I barred from asylum? A: Mandatory military service alone does not automatically bar you, but service in certain units — particularly the IRGC, which is a designated Foreign Terrorist Organization — can trigger statutory bars under INA § 208(b)(2), 8 U.S.C. § 1158(b)(2), including the persecutor bar and the terrorist organization bar. Some applicants have successfully argued duress (forced participation) as a defense. This is a complicated and fact-specific area of law. Consult an experienced asylum attorney before filing. Q: Will my family in Iran be at risk if I apply for asylum? A: This is a serious concern. Asylum filings in the U.S. are confidential by law (8 C.F.R. § 208.6) and cannot be disclosed to the Iranian government. However, Iran has a documented history of monitoring its diaspora and pressuring family members, and if your asylum claim later results in public proceedings (such as appeals), some information can become more visible. Many asylum seekers also avoid contacting the Iranian consulate after filing. Discuss your specific concerns with your attorney; they can request additional protective measures in some circumstances. Q: I left Iran on a passport that's now expired. Can I renew it? A: Renewing your Iranian passport requires you to interact with the Iranian Interest Section in Washington, D.C., or an Iranian consulate abroad. If you are an asylum applicant, this can complicate your case (USCIS may view it as evidence that you are not afraid to seek the Iranian government's protection). For non-asylum cases, it is your decision, but consult an immigration attorney first if you have any pending applications. [Heading2] Employment & Work Q: Can I still work while my case is pending? A: It depends on your specific situation. If you have a valid EAD, you can work. If your EAD is expiring with a pending renewal filed before October 30, 2025, you may qualify for an automatic extension. If you are on H-1B with a timely-filed extension, you may work under the 240-day rule. If you have an approved I-140 and pending I-485 (180+ days), you may have AC21 portability. Consult an attorney to confirm. Q: My employer is asking for updated work authorization documents — what should I provide? A: Use the IALDF Employer Letter template (separate downloadable document) to inform your HR department of your status and provide receipt notices for any pending applications. Federal law (8 U.S.C. § 1324b) prohibits document abuse — your employer cannot demand more or different documents than I-9 actually requires. Q: How do I know what I should or should not tell my employer or school about my immigration situation? A: Before communicating with your employer or school DSO about your immigration status, speak with an immigration attorney first. An attorney represents your interests, while your employer or school will typically prioritize their own compliance obligations. An attorney can help you determine what to share and how to frame the conversation. See the IALDF Employer Letter and School Letter templates.9 [Heading2] Students & OPT Q: What happens to my OPT if caught in the pause? A: Work closely with your DSO. If your OPT renewal was filed before October 30, 2025, the 540-day auto-extension may apply. If filed after, you generally cannot work without a final decision. Reach out to your DSO immediately. Q: My F-1 visa expires while my I-485 is pending — do I lose status? A: An expired visa is not the same as out-of-status. The F-1 visa stamp in your passport is for entry into the U.S.; once you are inside, your status is determined by your I-94 and your continued enrollment. A pending I-485 with an approved I-140 may also confer separate dual-intent benefits. Talk to your DSO and an immigration attorney about your specific situation. Q: If I want to apply for Day 1 CPT after my OPT ends, is that affected by the pause? A: No. Day 1 CPT involves a transfer of your SEVIS record to a new school program, which is not managed by USCIS and is therefore not subject to the pause.9 Q: Do I need to extend my F-1 status? A: As of April 2026, F-1 students are admitted for "duration of status," meaning your authorized stay is tied to your academic program rather than a fixed expiration date. You do not need to file an extension as long as you remain enrolled. However, a new rule is expected (not yet in effect) that would assign F-1 holders a specific expiration date instead.9 [Heading2] Naturalization Q: Will the pause delay my citizenship interview or oath ceremony? A: Yes, for nationals of designated countries — including Iran — N-400 final adjudications and oath ceremonies are paused. While your N-400 is pending, you remain a lawful permanent resident with all the rights that come with that status (you can work, travel under green-card rules, and renew your green card). Do not commit any criminal offenses or take long trips abroad while you wait. [Heading2] Legal Process Q: What is a mandamus action? A: A mandamus forces USCIS to make a decision. It does not guarantee approval — there is a risk your case may be denied sooner. Only pursue after careful consultation with an attorney. Q: Should I file an expedite request? A: Only if your situation fits one of USCIS's specific expedite criteria (severe financial loss, urgent humanitarian situation, nonprofit furthering U.S. cultural or social interests, government interest, or clear USCIS error) — and you can document it. See the IALDF USCIS Expedite Request template (separate downloadable document). A weak or unsupported expedite request can hurt your case. Q: Does my immigration case need to have already exceeded normal processing times to be eligible for litigation? A: Not necessarily. While unreasonable delay is one ground for mandamus litigation, there are other legal bases as well. Whether litigation is appropriate depends on the specific facts of your case. [Heading1] Section 8: Reference — Glossary Term Definition Adjustment of Status The process of applying for a green card while in the U.S., using Form I-485. Priority Date Your place in line for an immigrant visa — determined by when your employer filed PERM (employment-based) or when your I-130 was filed (family-based). Asylum Protection for individuals with a well-founded fear of persecution based on race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or social group. Filed on Form I-589. Mandamus A court action compelling USCIS to decide a pending case. Authorized under 5 U.S.C. § 706(1). Does not guarantee approval. Work Permit (EAD) Employment Authorization Document, requested through Form I-765. Green Card Permanent Resident Card — allows you to live, work, and study permanently in the U.S. Naturalization The process of becoming a U.S. citizen, typically via Form N-400 after 3–5 years as a permanent resident. USCIS U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services — the federal agency processing immigration applications. FBAR Report of Foreign Bank and Financial Accounts (FinCEN Form 114). Required for any U.S. person with aggregate foreign accounts over $10,000 at any point during the year — applies to all foreign countries, not just Iran. OFAC Office of Foreign Assets Control (U.S. Treasury). Administers economic and trade sanctions, including those affecting transactions involving Iran.
