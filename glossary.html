Frequently Asked Questions

These answers are general and may not fit your specific facts. Confirm with an immigration attorney before acting.

Updated as of April 30, 2026

General

What is the USCIS adjudication pause?

In December 2025 and January 2026, the Executive Branch issued two policy memoranda (PM-602-0192 and PM-602-0194) directing USCIS to pause adjudication of most immigration benefit applications for nationals of approximately 40 countries, including Iran. USCIS is not currently making decisions on pending cases from these countries.

Does IALDF provide legal representation?

No. IALDF does not provide legal representation or legal services. We provide resources, referrals, and advocacy support. If you need legal help, you will need to hire an attorney or find pro bono services.

I'm a U.S. citizen. Does the travel ban or pause affect me?

U.S. citizens cannot be denied entry to the United States because of national origin. The USCIS adjudication pause does not apply to applications you might have already — naturalized citizens are not subject to it. However, family-based petitions you file (such as an I-130 for an Iranian relative abroad) can be affected, because the beneficiary is from a designated country.

I hold dual nationality (Iranian and another foreign country). Does that exempt me from the pause?

No. USCIS uses country of origin to determine whether you fall under the travel-ban pause. Holding citizenship in a non-designated country does not exempt you if your country of origin is Iran or another designated country.

What can I do to be prepared in the unfortunate event of a sudden arrest?

Create an emergency preparedness plan. Keep original immigration documents on your person. Memorize the phone number of an immigration attorney. Prepare a plan that covers child care, finances, pet care, and emergency contacts in case you are suddenly unavailable. Share the plan with someone you trust. See the IALDF Family Preparedness Plan for a detailed template.

Travel & visas

Can I travel to Iran as a U.S. citizen or green card holder?

There is no U.S. legal prohibition on a citizen or permanent resident traveling to Iran, but the State Department has issued a Level 4 “Do Not Travel” advisory citing the risk of arbitrary arrest and detention of dual nationals. Iran does not recognize dual citizenship — if you enter Iran, the Iranian government may treat you exclusively as an Iranian citizen, including with respect to military service obligations. Talk to an immigration attorney before booking travel.

Can I leave the U.S. and re-enter while my application is pending?

Generally, no — leaving the U.S. while you have a pending I-485, asylum application, or some other benefit applications can be treated as abandonment unless you have an approved Advance Parole document (Form I-131). For F-1 and H-1B holders, separate travel rules apply. Always consult an attorney before international travel during the pause.

Can I travel internationally while the pause is in effect?

This is a case-by-case assessment that depends heavily on your immigration status and individual risk factors. Travel is currently riskier than usual because the State Department is actively revoking visas. There are two types of revocations: prudential (takes effect after you leave the U.S.) and immediate (your visa is revoked while you are here, which may subject you to deportation even with valid underlying status). Consult an immigration attorney before any international travel.

My visa expired but my extension is pending — am I out of status?

Not necessarily. If you filed your extension before your status expired, you are generally considered to be in a period of authorized stay while it is pending, even if your I-94 has expired. For H-1B holders, the 240-day rule allows you to keep working for the same employer for up to 240 days past the I-94 expiration date. Talk to your employer’s immigration counsel.

What should I tell CBP if asked about my Iranian citizenship?

Be truthful — making false statements to a federal officer is a federal crime. If you are a dual national, you can disclose that. If asked questions beyond your name and travel purpose that you are uncomfortable answering, you can say: “I prefer to answer further questions with an attorney present.” CBP cannot deny entry to a U.S. citizen but may delay you. For green card holders, refusing to answer can result in extended secondary inspection, but the government cannot revoke your status at the airport — only an immigration judge can do that.

Money & sanctions

Can I send money to family in Iran?

Yes, in most cases, through authorized channels. OFAC's general license at 31 C.F.R. § 560.545 authorizes non-commercial personal remittances to family members in Iran for basic living expenses, sent through a U.S. depository institution or U.S.-registered money transmitter. Some U.S. banks may decline to process Iran-related transactions even when they are legal. Keep documentation.

Will my U.S. bank close my account because I'm Iranian?

U.S. banks cannot lawfully close an account solely because of national origin — that is prohibited national-origin discrimination. However, banks have wide discretion to manage perceived sanctions or fraud risk and may close accounts citing those reasons. If a bank closes your account, ask in writing for the specific reason. If you believe national origin played a role, you can file a complaint with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (consumerfinance.gov) and consult a civil rights attorney.

Do I have to report my Iranian bank account on FBAR?

Yes — but this is not Iran-specific. FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) applies to any U.S. person with foreign financial accounts whose aggregate value exceeded $10,000 at any point during the year, in any country. The threshold and rule are the same whether the account is in Canada, the U.K., or Iran. Talk to a CPA or tax attorney with international reporting experience.

Can I inherit property in Iran?

U.S. sanctions law generally permits receipt of personal inheritance from family in Iran, but the transfer of funds or property may be subject to OFAC reporting or licensing requirements depending on the value, type of property, and timing. A sanctions attorney should review the specific facts before any transfer.

Asylum & Iran-specific risk

I served mandatory military service in Iran — am I barred from asylum?

Mandatory military service alone does not automatically bar you, but service in certain units — particularly the IRGC, which is a designated Foreign Terrorist Organization — can trigger statutory bars under INA § 208(b)(2), 8 U.S.C. § 1158(b)(2), including the persecutor bar and the terrorist organization bar. Some applicants have successfully argued duress (forced participation) as a defense. This is a complicated and fact-specific area of law. Consult an experienced asylum attorney before filing.

Will my family in Iran be at risk if I apply for asylum?

This is a serious concern. Asylum filings in the U.S. are confidential by law (8 C.F.R. § 208.6) and cannot be disclosed to the Iranian government. However, Iran has a documented history of monitoring its diaspora and pressuring family members, and if your asylum claim later results in public proceedings (such as appeals), some information can become more visible. Many asylum seekers also avoid contacting the Iranian consulate after filing. Discuss your specific concerns with your attorney; they can request additional protective measures in some circumstances.

I left Iran on a passport that's now expired. Can I renew it?

Renewing your Iranian passport requires you to interact with the Iranian Interest Section in Washington, D.C., or an Iranian consulate abroad. If you are an asylum applicant, this can complicate your case (USCIS may view it as evidence that you are not afraid to seek the Iranian government's protection). For non-asylum cases, it is your decision, but consult an immigration attorney first if you have any pending applications.

Employment & work

Can I still work while my case is pending?

It depends on your specific situation. If you have a valid EAD, you can work. If your EAD is expiring with a pending renewal filed before October 30, 2025, you may qualify for an automatic extension. If you are on H-1B with a timely-filed extension, you may work under the 240-day rule. If you have an approved I-140 and pending I-485 (180+ days), you may have AC21 portability. Consult an attorney to confirm.

My employer is asking for updated work authorization documents — what should I provide?

Use the IALDF Employer Letter template to inform your HR department of your status and provide receipt notices for any pending applications. Federal law (8 U.S.C. § 1324b) prohibits document abuse — your employer cannot demand more or different documents than I-9 actually requires.

How do I know what I should or should not tell my employer or school about my immigration situation?

Before communicating with your employer or school DSO about your immigration status, speak with an immigration attorney first. An attorney represents your interests, while your employer or school will typically prioritize their own compliance obligations. An attorney can help you determine what to share and how to frame the conversation. See the IALDF Employer Letter and School Letter templates.

Students & OPT

What happens to my OPT if caught in the pause?

Work closely with your DSO. If your OPT renewal was filed before October 30, 2025, the 540-day auto-extension may apply. If filed after, you generally cannot work without a final decision. Reach out to your DSO immediately.

My F-1 visa expires while my I-485 is pending — do I lose status?

An expired visa is not the same as out-of-status. The F-1 visa stamp in your passport is for entry into the U.S.; once you are inside, your status is determined by your I-94 and your continued enrollment. A pending I-485 with an approved I-140 may also confer separate dual-intent benefits. Talk to your DSO and an immigration attorney about your specific situation.

If I want to apply for Day-1 CPT after my OPT ends, is that affected by the pause?

No. Day-1 CPT involves a transfer of your SEVIS record to a new school program, which is not managed by USCIS and is therefore not subject to the pause.

Do I need to extend my F-1 status?

As of April 2026, F-1 students are admitted for “duration of status,” meaning your authorized stay is tied to your academic program rather than a fixed expiration date. You do not need to file an extension as long as you remain enrolled. However, a new rule is expected (not yet in effect) that would assign F-1 holders a specific expiration date instead.

Naturalization

Will the pause delay my citizenship interview or oath ceremony?

Yes, for nationals of designated countries — including Iran — N-400 final adjudications and oath ceremonies are paused. While your N-400 is pending, you remain a lawful permanent resident with all the rights that come with that status (you can work, travel under green-card rules, and renew your green card). Do not commit any criminal offenses or take long trips abroad while you wait.

Mental health & community support

I’m feeling overwhelmed by everything happening — is that normal?

Yes. The uncertainty surrounding immigration proceedings creates profound emotional strain. This stress is compounded by worry about family members in Iran, uncertainty about your own future, and separation from loved ones. These feelings are natural. You are not alone.

Legal process

What is a mandamus action?

A mandamus forces USCIS to make a decision. It does not guarantee approval — there is a risk your case may be denied sooner. Only pursue after careful consultation with an attorney.

Should I file an expedite request?

Only if your situation fits one of USCIS's specific expedite criteria (severe financial loss, urgent humanitarian situation, nonprofit furthering U.S. cultural or social interests, government interest, or clear USCIS error) — and you can document it. See the IALDF USCIS Expedite Request template. A weak or unsupported expedite request can hurt your case.

Does my immigration case need to have already exceeded normal processing times to be eligible for litigation?

Not necessarily. While unreasonable delay is one ground for mandamus litigation, there are other legal bases as well. Whether litigation is appropriate depends on the specific facts of your case. Consult an experienced immigration attorney to assess your options.
