Know Your Rights

As an Iranian American, understanding your legal rights is one of the most important things you can do to protect yourself and your family. Whether you are a U.S. citizen, a permanent resident, or here on a visa, certain fundamental protections apply to you under the Constitution and federal law.

Not legal advice

This is not legal advice. The information on this page is for general educational purposes only and is not a substitute for legal counsel. Consult a qualified immigration or civil-rights attorney about your specific situation.

Topics in detail

Your rights during police and ICE encounters

If you are stopped by law enforcement or by Immigration and Customs Enforcement, the most important thing to understand is that you have rights — and using those rights does not mean you have done anything wrong. Choosing to stay silent or to refuse a search cannot be used against you in court as evidence that you are guilty of something.

The Fourth Amendment protects you from unreasonable searches and seizures. The Fifth Amendment protects you from being forced to say things that could be used to incriminate yourself.1

Two kinds of warrants

A judicial warrant is signed by a judge and carries the full authority of the court. An administrative warrant — such as ICE Form I-200, signed by an ICE supervisor — does not carry the same legal weight. You are not legally required to open your door for an administrative warrant.2

What you can do

  • Remain calm and do not physically resist, even if you believe the stop is unlawful.
  • If an officer asks to search you or your belongings, say clearly: “I do not consent to a search.” Even if officers proceed, your verbal objection preserves your legal rights and may be important in court later.3
  • Ask: “Am I free to go?” If yes, walk away calmly. If no, you are being detained, and you should ask for an attorney.
  • If you are not free to go, say: “I want to speak to a lawyer.” Repeat if necessary. Once you have requested a lawyer, you are not required to answer further questions.

What you are required to provide — and to whom

If you are stopped by a local police officer, you are generally required to provide your name and, in some states, to show a state-issued ID if you have one. You are not required to answer questions about your immigration status, country of birth, or how you entered the United States. Local police in most jurisdictions do not have immigration enforcement authority.4

If you are stopped by an ICE or CBP agent, you must provide your name and show immigration documents if you have them (green card, visa, EAD). But you may refuse to answer substantive questions — where you were born, how you entered the country, your immigration history — by invoking your right to remain silent.5

Do not lie or present false documents. Making false statements to a federal agent is a federal crime, punishable by up to five years in prison.6

Stopped, detained, or arrested?

These terms have specific legal meanings, and the rights you have depend on which situation you are in.

SituationYour rights
Briefly stopped for questioning
An officer approaches you on the street, in a car, or at your workplace.		 You must provide your name if asked. You may ask “Am I free to go?” If yes, leave calmly. You do not have to answer questions about your immigration status (unless the officer is ICE or CBP).
Stopped & questioned by ICE or CBP
At a checkpoint, workplace raid, or your home.		 You must provide your name and immigration documents if you have them. You may decline to answer substantive questions. Say: “I am exercising my right to remain silent. I would like to speak to a lawyer.”
Detained (not free to leave, but not formally arrested)
If the officer says you are not free to leave, you are detained.		 You have the right to remain silent. You may ask “Why am I being detained?” You do not have a right to a government-appointed attorney during a brief investigative detention, but you may request to contact one. Do not sign anything without legal advice.
Arrested (formally placed in custody)
You are told you are under arrest, placed in handcuffs, or read your rights.		 You have the right to remain silent. You have the right to know the charges. In criminal proceedings, one will be appointed if you cannot afford one. In immigration proceedings, you must obtain your own attorney. Say: “I want to speak to a lawyer” and remain silent.
Your rights at home

Your home receives the strongest protection under the Fourth Amendment. As a general rule, law enforcement needs a warrant signed by a judge to enter your home.10

What you can do at the door

  • You do not have to open the door. Ask: “Do you have a warrant?” If they say yes, ask to see it — held to a window or slid under the door — before opening.
  • If there is no judicial warrant, you may say: “I do not consent to entry. Please leave.” An ICE administrative warrant (Form I-200) does not compel you to open your door.
  • Even with a valid judicial warrant, ask: “What is this warrant for?” Cooperate, but do not volunteer additional information.
  • If officers claim an emergency, they may enter without a warrant under the “exigent circumstances” exception — for example, if they are pursuing someone they have reason to believe just committed a serious crime, or someone inside is in immediate danger.
Read the warrant before the door opens

Ask officers to slide the warrant under the door or hold it up to a window so you can read it before letting anyone inside. Check whether it is signed by a judge (judicial) or by an immigration officer (administrative). An ICE administrative warrant — Form I-200 — does not compel you to open the door.

If others are home

Make sure everyone in your household — including children old enough to understand — knows the basic rules: do not open the door, do not answer questions, ask for a lawyer. Practice the conversation as a family. The Family Preparedness Plan in the Templates section includes a worksheet for this.

At airports and the border

Airports and border crossings are legally distinct from other locations. Customs and Border Protection officers have broader authority to stop, question, and search travelers than police have during a routine street encounter. You still have rights.

When entering the United States

If you are a U.S. citizen (including naturalized citizens), you have the right to enter the United States. You must present a valid passport or other acceptable travel document. Declining to answer questions may cause delay but cannot ultimately prevent a citizen from entering.

Green-card holders have the right to return after travel abroad, provided the green card has not expired and there are no disqualifying criminal convictions. The government cannot revoke your status at the airport — only an immigration judge can do that.

Key phrases for CBP encounters

  • CBP asks questions beyond your name and travel purpose
    “I prefer to answer further questions with an attorney present.”
  • CBP asks you to consent to a search
    “I do not consent to a search.” CBP may search anyway, but your objection is noted.
  • Pulled aside for secondary inspection
    “Am I being detained or am I free to go? I would like to contact an attorney.”

Consular notification: a serious decision

If you are detained or arrested, you may be asked whether you wish to have your country’s consulate notified. Under the Vienna Convention on Consular Relations, U.S. authorities are generally required to inform you of this right.17

Consular notification carries risk

Before requesting that the Iranian consulate (or the Iranian Interest Section) be notified, carefully consider the potential consequences. Iran has a documented history of monitoring its diaspora communities and nationals abroad. Requesting consular notification creates an official record that the Iranian government could access. While the specific risk to family members in Iran is difficult to quantify, the possibility exists and should be discussed with your attorney before you make this decision. If you are uncertain, tell the officers: “I need to speak with my attorney before making a decision about consular notification.”

Military service obligations

Iran requires military service for male citizens. Males are generally subject to service obligations until approximately age 49–50, though specific rules and exceptions apply under Iranian law.18

If you are an Iranian national or dual national who has not completed military service, returning to Iran could result in your detention at the border. You may be prevented from leaving Iran until your obligation is fulfilled or a financial penalty is paid. Deferments are available for full-time university students, but the deferment is temporary — once you graduate or withdraw, the service obligation resumes. Consult an immigration attorney before planning any travel to Iran.

Travel to Iran — security advisory

The U.S. State Department has issued a Level 4 “Do Not Travel” advisory for Iran, citing the risk of arbitrary arrest and detention of dual nationals and the limited ability of the U.S. government to provide consular assistance if you are detained.19

Iran does not recognize dual citizenship

If you enter Iran, the Iranian government may treat you exclusively as an Iranian citizen, including with respect to military service obligations. U.S. citizenship will not protect you inside Iran.

Discrimination — your legal protections

Federal and state laws protect you from discrimination based on your race, national origin, religion, immigration status, and other characteristics. These protections apply broadly — at work, in housing, at school, and in public places.

Where the law protects you

  • At work. Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 prohibits employers from refusing to hire, firing, or mistreating you because of your race, national origin, or religion.13
  • In housing. The Fair Housing Act prohibits discrimination in rental, sales, lending, and other housing-related services based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, disability, and family status.14
  • In public places. Title II of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 protects your right to equal service in hotels, restaurants, stores, and public transportation.15
  • At school. Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 prohibits discrimination in schools and programs receiving federal funds.16

If you experience discrimination

  • Keep a detailed record of any evidence — emails, texts, photos, dates, times, and witness names.
  • Report it to the right agency.
    • Employment discrimination → EEOC (Equal Employment Opportunity Commission)
    • Housing discrimination → HUD (Department of Housing and Urban Development)
    • School discrimination → Department of Education, Office for Civil Rights
  • Consult an attorney. Many lawyers offer free initial consultations for discrimination cases.
A bank cannot close your account just because you are Iranian

U.S. banks cannot lawfully close an account solely because of national origin — that would be prohibited national-origin discrimination. However, banks have wide discretion to manage perceived risk and may close accounts citing those reasons. If a bank closes your account, ask in writing for the specific reason. If you believe national origin played a role, file a complaint with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (consumerfinance.gov) and consult a civil-rights attorney.

U.S. sanctions on Iran

The U.S. government restricts most financial dealings with Iran. The rules are run by a U.S. Treasury office called the Office of Foreign Assets Control (most lawyers call it OFAC). For most everyday situations — sending money to a parent in Tehran, holding a savings account in Iran, reporting it on your U.S. taxes — there are clear, legal paths. The rules are explained here in plain English.20

Sending money to family in Iran

Yes, this is usually legal. The Treasury Department issues something called a “general license” for personal remittances — a standing permission slip you don’t need to apply for. As long as you send the money through a U.S. bank or a registered money-transfer service, and the money is for basic living expenses for a family member, it’s allowed.21

Some U.S. banks may still decline Iran-related transfers, even when they’re legal, because they want to avoid any sanctions risk. If that happens, try another bank or a registered money-transmitter. Keep documentation of who you sent the money to and what it was for.

What about goods?

You generally cannot import goods from Iran or export goods to Iran without applying for a specific license from the Treasury Department. This includes carpets, art, antiques, and other items. Inheritance of personal property from family in Iran is often allowed, but the transfer of funds or items may need Treasury approval depending on value and timing. A sanctions attorney should review the specific facts before any transfer.

Travel to Iran — is it legal?

U.S. sanctions law does not categorically prohibit U.S. citizens and permanent residents from traveling to Iran. Many Iranian Americans do visit family. However, certain travel-related transactions may need authorization depending on the purpose of the trip. The bigger concern is the State Department’s Level 4 “Do Not Travel” advisory and the risk of arbitrary detention by Iranian authorities — see the airports & borders section above.22

What is FBAR? (Foreign-account reporting)

If you have any foreign financial account — in Iran or anywhere else — and the combined value crosses $10,000 at any point during the year, you must file a report called FBAR (FinCEN Form 114). This is a yearly filing with the U.S. Treasury.

FBAR is not an Iran rule

FBAR applies to every foreign country — a savings account in Canada or the U.K. triggers the same filing. It is not an extra burden placed on Iranian Americans. What is Iran-specific: certain property interests in Iran may be subject to separate Treasury reporting or blocking requirements. If you have accounts or property abroad, talk to a CPA or tax attorney with international experience.23

If you are uncertain

  • Consult an attorney who specializes in sanctions and export-control law.
  • Check the Treasury Department’s OFAC website (treasury.gov/ofac) for current regulations and FAQs.
  • Keep documentation of every transaction involving Iran.

The USCIS Adjudication Pause

In December 2025 and January 2026, USCIS issued two policy memos — PM-602-0192 and PM-602-0194 — implementing a broad pause on final decisions for immigration applications from individuals from roughly 40 countries, including Iran.1

Not legal advice

The information contained in this site is provided for informational purposes only, and should not be construed as legal advice on any subject matter. Immigration policy is changing rapidly during the pause. Consult an immigration attorney before making any decisions about your case.

What is happening

For most types of applications — including student visas (F-1), work visas (H-1B), green card applications, and asylum — USCIS is still accepting applications and conducting interviews, but is not issuing final approvals or denials.

On March 30, 2026, USCIS announced that it would lift its pause on processing applications from “thoroughly screened asylum seekers from non-high-risk countries.” But USCIS has not defined which countries it considers “high-risk” and has not provided written guidance on this policy change. The pause continues for individuals from travel-ban countries, including Iran.2

Re-review of previously approved benefits

USCIS is conducting a re-review of benefit requests that were approved on or after January 20, 2021, for individuals from designated countries. The date that matters is when USCIS approved your benefits — not when you entered the United States. Officers are also conducting significantly increased interview scrutiny: interviews are taking longer and officers are asking detailed questions about intent and background.

Who is affected

USCIS uses country of origin — not current citizenship — to determine whether you fall under the pause. Holding dual nationality with a non-designated country does not exempt you. For example, Canadian citizens filing for TN visas face the same pause if their country of origin is a designated country.3

  • All asylum seekers are affected regardless of nationality.
  • For nationals of designated countries including Iran, all USCIS-administered benefits are paused, and approvals on or after January 20, 2021 are subject to re-review.
  • Humanitarian benefits for nationals of travel-ban countries are also paused without exception — U visas, VAWA self-petitions, I-751 waivers, humanitarian parole.

OPT & STEM OPT (Optional Practical Training)

OPT applications (Form I-765) are subject to the pause. You can still file, but USCIS is not issuing final decisions.

Auto-extension rules changed Oct 30, 2025. If you filed your renewal before that date, you may be eligible for up to a 540-day automatic extension and can continue working. If you filed after, most applicants no longer receive an automatic extension and cannot work without a final USCIS decision.3

Initial OPT (not a renewal) has never qualified for the 540-day extension — you cannot start working until USCIS issues your EAD.

STEM OPT: If you timely filed your 24-month STEM OPT extension, the 180-day automatic extension of your current work authorization continues to operate normally and is not affected by the pause.4 Your authorization expires at the end of 180 days if USCIS has not decided — many students are at risk of reaching that limit.

Court development: On April 17, 2026, a federal court in Behdin v. Edlow issued a preliminary injunction requiring USCIS to adjudicate certain Form I-765 applications. The ruling is limited to the 32 named plaintiffs but may provide grounds for similarly situated individuals to pursue their own litigation.5

What you should do: Talk to your DSO immediately. Keep all documentation. Know your critical deadlines (I-20, OPT, STEM-OPT 180-day limit). Explore SEVIS transfer or CPT placement. Consult an attorney about mandamus litigation if your status is at risk.

F-1 Student Status

Your F-1 status continues as long as you maintain a full course of study and comply with your school’s requirements. A delay in an OPT application does not by itself terminate your F-1 status.

SEVIS transfers continue. You can transfer your SEVIS record from one school to another — the pause affects final decisions on benefit requests, not the SEVIS system itself.6 This includes Day-1 CPT transfers after OPT ends.

Duration of status. F-1 students are currently admitted for “duration of status” — your authorized stay is tied to your academic program rather than a fixed date. You do not need to file a status extension as long as you remain enrolled. A new rule is expected (not yet in effect) that would assign F-1 holders a specific expiration date instead.

CPT trade-off: Completing 12 months or more of full-time CPT disqualifies you from OPT. Discuss this with your DSO before enrolling.7

60-day grace period: If your program is ending, act before this grace period runs out.8

J-1 Exchange Visitor Status

J-1 status is governed by the dates on your DS-2019 and your program sponsor’s approval. The pause affects some J-1 applications, but your underlying status is tied to your sponsor. Contact your program sponsor directly for guidance.

You cannot transfer a J-1 program to a different program category — transfers must remain within the same category. A category change would require a new application and would be subject to the pause.

What to ask your sponsor: Is my DS-2019 still in good standing and when does it expire? Do I need a USCIS-administered benefit that would be subject to the pause? What does the 30-day grace period after my program end date look like for my situation?

H-1B Work Visa

H-1B holders have two protections most other visa types do not have during the pause.

240-day extension rule. If your H-1B extension is pending and you filed it before your current H-1B expired, you may continue working for your current employer for up to 240 days past your expiration. This only applies if you filed timely.9

Portability. H-1B holders can transfer to a new employer without being affected by the pause. H-1B portability operates under a separate statutory framework that does not require the pending petition to be adjudicated before you begin work for the new employer. This does not apply to O-1 or L-1.

Change-of-status applications — for example, F-1 to H-1B — are not being adjudicated. If you are maintaining valid status in one category, consider preserving it rather than applying for a change that will not be processed.

EAD auto-extension: The Oct 30, 2025 rule change affects EAD holders in employment-based categories (A03, A05, A10, C08, C09, C26, C31). Renewals filed on or after that date generally do not get auto-extension. Exceptions: TPS beneficiaries (A12/C19) if announced via Federal Register, STEM OPT (180-day), and spouses of L and E visa holders.10

Employment-Based Green Card (PERM, I-140, I-485)

AC21 portability. If your employer’s I-140 is approved and your I-485 has been pending for at least 180 days, you may be able to change employers without losing your place in line. This is called portability under AC21.11

Both boxes must be checked: an approved I-140 and an I-485 pending 180+ days. Talk to immigration counsel before any employer change.

Risk to watch: If your I-485 is pending and your underlying status has expired or is about to expire, ICE is no longer consistently following prior precedent that protected you from accruing unlawful presence. There have been cases where Iranians with pending I-485 applications were approached by ICE, detained, and placed in removal proceedings after their underlying status expired. Consult an immigration attorney immediately if your underlying status is expiring while your I-485 is pending.

Asylum Seekers & Detainees

The pause affects affirmative asylum applications (filed with USCIS) for Iranians. Work permits for pending asylum seekers (category C08) are also affected.

If you have a pending asylum application but have never previously filed for employment authorization, your initial EAD based on your pending asylum case is not subject to the pause.

One-year asylum filing deadline

Federal law requires you to file your asylum application within one year of arriving in the U.S. This deadline is NOT waived by the pause. If you miss it, you may permanently lose the right to apply for asylum. Limited exceptions exist for changed or extraordinary circumstances, but they are narrowly construed. File as early as possible.12

Your protections during the pause:

  • Credible-fear interview in expedited removal proceedings.13
  • Withholding of removal & CAT protection remain available even if you missed the one-year deadline.14
  • Bond hearing rights if you are detained.15
  • Do not agree to voluntary departure without consulting an attorney.16

Iran-specific: Common asylum grounds for Iranians include political opinion, religion or conversion from Islam, ethnicity, gender-based persecution, and LGBTQ+ identity.

IRGC service. If you served in the Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps (or certain other units), your asylum claim may face statutory bars: the persecutor bar and the terrorist-organization bar (the IRGC is a designated Foreign Terrorist Organization). Even mandatory or conscripted service can raise these issues. An experienced asylum attorney is essential.17

Document procurement: Many Iranians cannot obtain documents from the Iranian government. Immigration courts apply a “whole person” assessment and may accept alternative evidence when documents are genuinely impossible to obtain. The burden is on you to explain why.

Do not contact the Iranian consulate during an asylum claim

Doing so could alert the Iranian government to your claim. Consult an attorney before contacting any government agency.

Green Card Holders (conditional & permanent residents)

If you are a permanent resident, you have the right to re-enter the United States after travel abroad, provided your green card has not expired and you do not have disqualifying criminal convictions or immigration violations.18

  • Your green card remains valid for its full term. The pause does not invalidate or shorten it.
  • You have constitutional due process protections that do not go away during the pause.
  • Form I-90 renewals and replacements are not subject to the pause — USCIS will process them.
  • There is no mandatory requirement to apply for citizenship. You may hold your green card indefinitely.
Approvals on or after January 20, 2021

If your green card was approved on or after January 20, 2021, it may be subject to re-review and possible re-interview under the December 2025 memo. Immigration attorneys report that reopened cases have so far been relatively limited and have primarily involved individuals whose military service was affiliated with the IRGC, or situations where USCIS determined insufficient information was gathered during the original interview.

What to do: Renew your green card before it expires using Form I-90. If you are a conditional resident (2-year green card), file Form I-751 on time, even during the pause. Carry your green card at all times. Do not travel for extended periods (6+ months) without consulting an attorney.

Naturalization (N-400)

N-400 applications from Iranian nationals are subject to the pause. Oath ceremonies and final decisions are delayed.

No oath-ceremony exception for Iranians. The exception that allows oath ceremonies to proceed during the pause does not apply to Iranian nationals. As of April 20, 2026, Iranians whose ceremonies were scheduled and subsequently canceled cannot complete their naturalization — even though applicants from non-designated countries may still proceed under the exception.

If you are a U.S. citizen and your child holds a green card and is under 21, Form N-600 (Certificate of Citizenship) is not subject to the pause.

While your naturalization is pending, you remain a lawful permanent resident with all associated rights.19

What to do: Keep documentation of continuous residence and physical presence. Do not commit any criminal offenses. Do not travel for extended periods — extensive travel can call into question continuous residence.

Pending litigation — five federal courts have ruled against the pause

As of April 22, 2026, five federal district courts have entered orders blocking PM-602-0192 and PM-602-0194 as applied to the plaintiffs before them. The hold remains in effect nationwide for everyone else. Every court that has reached the legal merits has held plaintiffs are likely to succeed; no court has upheld the hold on the merits.

Orders granting relief:

  • Behdin v. Edlow (N.D. Cal., Apr 15–16, 2026) — Preliminary injunction for 32 named plaintiffs (31 Iranian, 1 Sudanese). The most analytically developed order; identifies four independent grounds for relief.
  • Shokri Varniab v. Edlow (N.D. Cal., Feb 20, 2026) — Two plaintiffs, 30-day adjudication deadline.
  • Bowser v. Noem (D. Mass., Feb 27, 2026) — One plaintiff, 10-day EAD adjudication deadline (the most aggressive remedy in any pause-related case).
  • Doe v. USCIS (N.D. Ill., Mar 26, 2026) — TRO covering one plaintiff’s I-485, I-765, I-131.
  • Doe v. Trump (D. Mass., Apr 16–17, 2026) — Temporary stay before full PI briefing.

The one adverse ruling: Osman v. USCIS (E.D. Mich., Apr 15, 2026) — PI denied on irreparable-harm grounds; court did not reach the legal merits. Plaintiff sought I-131 advance parole only.

Should you sue? A mandamus action compels USCIS to make a decision — it does not guarantee approval. By forcing your case to be adjudicated, there is a risk that it may be denied sooner than it otherwise would have been. Discuss the risks thoroughly with an immigration attorney before pursuing litigation.

Among the many pause-related lawsuits filed to date, outcomes have been strongly favorable for applicants. If you’re considering litigation, work with an attorney very familiar with Iranian cases who can assess your specific risk factors — sanctioned-industry employment, field of study, academic and professional connections, family members in Iran, military-service history. Heightened scrutiny requires what attorneys call “extreme consistency” across every interaction you’ve had with the immigration system.

What you should do — and not do

Do not file for B-2 tourist status

Some immigration attorneys advise NOT filing for B-2 (tourist visa) status as a fallback strategy. Under the current administration, B-2 status is not a safe backup for individuals on other visa types. Filing for a B-2 extension or change of status can trigger heightened scrutiny of your entire immigration file and may actually accelerate removal proceedings rather than buy you time.

Keep your documents on you

More than ever, it is important to keep original evidence of your immigration status on your person at all times. Depending on your situation: your green card, approval notices for your current status, receipt notices for any pending extensions, your passport with visa stamp, your I-20 if you are a student, your EAD. Workers should also carry recent pay statements, an offer letter, and a recently signed verification-of-employment letter. Students should carry transcripts.

For all important documents — birth certificates, immigration filings, financial records — keep originals in a secure location and make sure someone you trust knows where to find them.

Memorize your attorney’s phone number

Memorize the phone number of an immigration attorney you have worked with, and share that number with someone you trust so they know who to call if something happens to you. Get to know an immigration lawyer before any problems arise — having an established relationship can be critical in an emergency.

Talk to an attorney before you talk to your employer or school

Before communicating with your employer or school about your immigration situation, consider speaking with an immigration attorney first. An attorney represents your interests, whereas your employer or school will typically prioritize their own compliance obligations and institutional interests. See the Employer Letter and School Letter templates below for guidance — but talk to counsel before sending anything.

Prepare for emergencies

Prepare for the possibility of being suddenly detained with no access to the outside world for at least a short period. An emergency plan should cover emergency contacts, how rent and bills will be paid, care for children or pets, and access to your finances. This is especially important if you are single. The Family Preparedness Plan below is a comprehensive template for this.

Frequently Asked Questions

General questions, travel and visas, money and sanctions, asylum, work, students, naturalization, and the legal process. These answers are general and may not fit your specific facts — confirm with an immigration attorney before acting.

Not legal advice

These answers are general. Confirm with an immigration attorney before acting on your specific situation.

General

What is the USCIS adjudication pause?

In December 2025 and January 2026, the Executive Branch issued two policy memoranda (PM-602-0192 and PM-602-0194) directing USCIS to pause adjudication of most immigration benefit applications for nationals of approximately 40 countries, including Iran. USCIS is not currently making decisions on pending cases from these countries.

Does IALDF provide legal representation?

No. IALDF does not provide legal representation or legal services. We provide resources, referrals, and advocacy support. If you need legal help, you will need to hire an attorney or find pro bono services.

I’m a U.S. citizen. Does the travel ban or pause affect me?

U.S. citizens cannot be denied entry to the United States because of national origin. The USCIS adjudication pause does not apply to applications you might have already — naturalized citizens are not subject to it. However, family-based petitions you file (such as an I-130 for an Iranian relative abroad) can be affected, because the beneficiary is from a designated country.

I hold dual nationality. Does that exempt me from the pause?

No. USCIS uses country of origin — not current citizenship — to determine whether you fall under the travel-ban pause. Holding citizenship in a non-designated country does not exempt you if your country of origin is Iran or another designated country.

What can I do to be prepared in the event of a sudden arrest?

Create an emergency preparedness plan. Keep original immigration documents on your person. Memorize the phone number of an immigration attorney. Prepare a plan that covers child care, finances, pet care, and emergency contacts. Share the plan with someone you trust. See the Family Preparedness Plan below.

Travel & visas

Can I travel to Iran as a U.S. citizen or green card holder?

There is no U.S. legal prohibition, but the State Department has issued a Level 4 “Do Not Travel” advisory citing the risk of arbitrary arrest and detention of dual nationals. Iran does not recognize dual citizenship — if you enter Iran, the Iranian government may treat you exclusively as an Iranian citizen, including with respect to military-service obligations. Talk to an immigration attorney before booking travel.

Can I leave the U.S. and re-enter while my application is pending?

Generally, no — leaving the U.S. while you have a pending I-485, asylum application, or some other benefit applications can be treated as abandonment unless you have an approved Advance Parole (Form I-131). For F-1 and H-1B holders, separate travel rules apply. Always consult an attorney before international travel during the pause.

Can I travel internationally while the pause is in effect?

Case-by-case. Travel is currently riskier than usual because the State Department is actively revoking visas. Two types: prudential (takes effect after you leave the U.S.) and immediate (revoked while you’re here, which may subject you to deportation even with valid underlying status). Consult an immigration attorney before any international travel.

My visa expired but my extension is pending — am I out of status?

Not necessarily. If you filed your extension before your status expired, you are generally considered to be in a period of authorized stay while it is pending, even if your I-94 has expired. For H-1B holders, the 240-day rule allows you to keep working for the same employer for up to 240 days past the I-94 expiration. Talk to your employer’s immigration counsel.

What should I tell CBP if asked about my Iranian citizenship?

Be truthful — making false statements to a federal officer is a federal crime. If you are a dual national, you can disclose that. If asked questions beyond your name and travel purpose, you can say: “I prefer to answer further questions with an attorney present.” CBP cannot deny entry to a U.S. citizen but may delay you. For green card holders, refusing to answer can result in extended secondary inspection, but the government cannot revoke your status at the airport — only an immigration judge can do that.

Money & sanctions

Can I send money to family in Iran?

Yes, in most cases, through authorized channels. A Treasury general license at 31 C.F.R. § 560.545 authorizes non-commercial personal remittances to family members in Iran for basic living expenses, sent through a U.S. bank or U.S.-registered money transmitter. Some U.S. banks may decline Iran-related transactions even when legal. Keep documentation.

Will my U.S. bank close my account because I’m Iranian?

U.S. banks cannot lawfully close an account solely because of national origin — that is prohibited national-origin discrimination. However, banks have wide discretion to manage perceived sanctions or fraud risk and may close accounts citing those reasons. If a bank closes your account, ask in writing for the specific reason. If you believe national origin played a role, file a complaint with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (consumerfinance.gov) and consult a civil-rights attorney.

Do I have to report my Iranian bank account on FBAR?

Yes — but this is not Iran-specific. FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) applies to any U.S. person with foreign financial accounts whose aggregate value exceeded $10,000 at any point during the year, in any country. The threshold and rule are the same whether the account is in Canada, the U.K., or Iran. Talk to a CPA or tax attorney with international experience.

Can I inherit property in Iran?

U.S. sanctions law generally permits receipt of personal inheritance from family in Iran, but the transfer of funds or property may be subject to Treasury reporting or licensing requirements depending on value, type of property, and timing. A sanctions attorney should review the specific facts before any transfer.

Asylum & Iran-specific risk

I served mandatory military service in Iran — am I barred from asylum?

Mandatory military service alone does not automatically bar you, but service in certain units — particularly the IRGC, which is a designated Foreign Terrorist Organization — can trigger statutory bars under INA § 208(b)(2), including the persecutor bar and the terrorist-organization bar. Some applicants have successfully argued duress (forced participation) as a defense. This is a complicated and fact-specific area. Consult an experienced asylum attorney before filing.

Will my family in Iran be at risk if I apply for asylum?

This is a serious concern. Asylum filings in the U.S. are confidential by law (8 C.F.R. § 208.6) and cannot be disclosed to the Iranian government. However, Iran has a documented history of monitoring its diaspora and pressuring family members, and if your asylum claim later results in public proceedings, some information can become more visible. Many asylum seekers also avoid contacting the Iranian consulate after filing. Discuss your specific concerns with your attorney.

I left Iran on a passport that’s now expired. Can I renew it?

Renewing your Iranian passport requires you to interact with the Iranian Interest Section in Washington, D.C., or an Iranian consulate abroad. If you are an asylum applicant, this can complicate your case (USCIS may view it as evidence that you are not afraid to seek the Iranian government’s protection). For non-asylum cases, it is your decision, but consult an immigration attorney first if you have any pending applications.

Employment, students, naturalization

Can I still work while my case is pending?

It depends. If you have a valid EAD, you can work. If your EAD is expiring with a pending renewal filed before October 30, 2025, you may qualify for an automatic extension. If you are on H-1B with a timely-filed extension, you may work under the 240-day rule. If you have an approved I-140 and pending I-485 (180+ days), you may have AC21 portability. Consult an attorney to confirm.

My employer is asking for updated work-authorization documents — what should I provide?

Use the Employer Letter template to inform your HR department of your status and provide receipt notices for any pending applications. Federal law (8 U.S.C. § 1324b) prohibits document abuse — your employer cannot demand more or different documents than I-9 actually requires.

What happens to my OPT if caught in the pause?

Work closely with your DSO. If your OPT renewal was filed before October 30, 2025, the 540-day auto-extension may apply. If filed after, you generally cannot work without a final decision. Reach out to your DSO immediately.

If I want to apply for Day-1 CPT after my OPT ends, is that affected by the pause?

No. Day-1 CPT involves a transfer of your SEVIS record to a new school program, which is not managed by USCIS and is therefore not subject to the pause.

Will the pause delay my citizenship interview or oath ceremony?

Yes, for nationals of designated countries including Iran — N-400 final adjudications and oath ceremonies are paused. While your N-400 is pending, you remain a lawful permanent resident with all associated rights. Do not commit any criminal offenses or take long trips abroad while you wait.

Legal process

What is a mandamus action?

A mandamus forces USCIS to make a decision. It does not guarantee approval — there is a risk your case may be denied sooner. Only pursue after careful consultation with an attorney.

Should I file an expedite request?

Only if your situation fits one of USCIS’s specific expedite criteria — severe financial loss, urgent humanitarian situation, nonprofit furthering U.S. cultural or social interests, government interest, or clear USCIS error — and you can document it. See the USCIS Expedite Request template. A weak or unsupported request can hurt your case.

Does my case need to have exceeded normal processing times to be eligible for litigation?

Not necessarily. While unreasonable delay is one ground for mandamus litigation, there are other legal bases as well. Whether litigation is appropriate depends on the specific facts of your case. Consult an experienced immigration attorney to assess your options.

Behdin’s four APA grounds on the USCIS adjudication pause

Judge Van Keulen identified four independent APA grounds in Behdin v. Edlow (N.D. Cal., Apr. 15–16, 2026) — the most thorough legal analysis of the freeze to date. Any one of these grounds would sustain a preliminary injunction.

  1. Unreasonable delay under § 706(1), applying the six-factor TRAC framework.

    The APA gives courts power to order agencies to act when they have sat on applications too long. The freeze is open-ended with no timeline, no conditions for ending, and no acknowledgment of how long is acceptable. For EAD applications specifically, the INA sets a 90-day deadline that the freeze directly violates.

  2. Unlawful withholding under § 706(1) — refusal to perform a legal duty.

    Under both the INA and APA, USCIS is legally required to process properly filed applications — this is not optional. The freeze crosses the line from delay into an outright refusal to carry out a legal duty. USCIS has discretion in evaluating applications but does not have the option to stop deciding them.

  3. Arbitrary and capricious action under § 706(2)(A), applying State Farm and Regents.

    The APA requires agencies to explain their reasoning and weigh real-world impact when changing rules. The freeze was imposed without any explanation of why these specific countries were chosen. The government never acknowledged the harm or considered less drastic options such as additional security screening rather than a blanket halt.

  4. Failure to follow notice-and-comment under § 553.

    The APA requires publishing proposed rules, opening a public comment period, and responding to submissions before changing rules that affect legal rights. None of that happened — the freeze was imposed through internal memos. Courts found the government cannot fix this after the fact; the agency would have to cancel the freeze and restart the full process.

The § 553 finding is the most consequential — if affirmed, USCIS would have to rescind the PMs and re-promulgate any similar policy through formal rulemaking. Vacatur, not remand, would be the remedy.

Practice notes

  • Standalone I-131 claims are significantly harder to sustain on irreparable harm than EAD or AOS claims. Consider bundling with an I-765 or I-485 claim, as Judge Ellis permitted in the N.D. Ill. TRO.
  • Forum selection. Two of five wins are from Judge Van Keulen (N.D. Cal.); two are from D. Mass. E.D. Mich. and N.D. Ga. are the districts most likely to yield a government-favorable ruling.
  • Iran-specific risk factors. Sanctioned-industry employment, field of study, academic and professional connections, family members in Iran and their occupations, and military-service history (especially IRGC). Heightened scrutiny requires what attorneys are calling “extreme consistency” across all prior filings.
  • FBI background checks now encompass all interactions with law enforcement or any government agency throughout the client’s life, inside and outside the United States. Advise clients not to speak with federal investigators about their case or personal history without counsel present.

Glossary

Quick definitions for the acronyms and forms that come up most often across this page.

240-Day Rule
A regulation allowing H-1B workers to continue working for the same employer for up to 240 days past expiration if a timely extension was filed. 8 C.F.R. § 214.2(h)(15)(ii)(D).
AC21 Portability
A provision allowing employment-based green-card applicants to change employers without losing their place in line, once their I-140 is approved and I-485 has been pending 180+ days. INA § 204(j).
Adjustment of Status
The process of applying for a green card while in the U.S., using Form I-485.
Asylum
Protection for individuals with a well-founded fear of persecution based on race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or social group. Filed on Form I-589.
Credible Fear Interview
A screening conducted by USCIS to determine whether a person in expedited removal has a credible fear of persecution or torture if returned to their country. 8 C.F.R. § 208.30.
FBAR
Report of Foreign Bank and Financial Accounts (FinCEN Form 114). Required for any U.S. person with aggregate foreign accounts over $10,000 at any point during the year — applies to all foreign countries, not just Iran.
Green Card
Permanent Resident Card — allows you to live, work, and study permanently in the U.S.
Judicial vs. Administrative Warrant
A judicial warrant is signed by a judge and carries the full authority of the court. An administrative warrant (e.g., ICE Form I-200) is issued by an immigration officer and does not compel you to open your door.
Mandamus
A court action compelling USCIS to decide a pending case. Authorized under 5 U.S.C. § 706(1). Does not guarantee approval.
Naturalization
The process of becoming a U.S. citizen, typically via Form N-400 after 3–5 years as a permanent resident.
OFAC
Office of Foreign Assets Control — the U.S. Treasury office that administers economic and trade sanctions, including those affecting transactions involving Iran.
Priority Date
Your place in line for an immigrant visa — determined by when your employer filed PERM (employment-based) or when your I-130 was filed (family-based).
USCIS
U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services — the federal agency processing immigration applications.
Withholding of Removal / CAT
Protections from removal that remain available even when asylum is not — including under the Convention Against Torture. Lower standard of relief than asylum.
Work Permit (EAD)
Employment Authorization Document, requested through Form I-765.
