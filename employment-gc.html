How the pause affects asylum

On March 30, 2026, USCIS announced it would lift the pause on processing affirmative asylum applications from “thoroughly screened asylum seekers from non-high-risk countries.” However, USCIS has not defined which countries it considers “high-risk” and has not provided written guidance. The pause continues for nationals of travel-ban countries, including Iran. Work permits for pending asylum seekers (category C08) remain affected.

If you have a pending asylum application but have never previously filed for employment authorization, your initial employment authorization application based on your pending asylum case is not subject to the pause.8

The one-year filing deadline

One-year asylum filing deadline

Federal law requires you to file your asylum application within one year of arriving in the United States. This deadline is NOT waived by the adjudication pause. If you miss this deadline, you may permanently lose the right to apply for asylum.

File as early as possible — do not wait. Limited exceptions exist for “changed circumstances” or “extraordinary circumstances” (such as a material change in conditions in your home country), but these are narrowly construed. Consult an attorney to determine if an exception applies.

Your protections

  • Right to a credible-fear interview. If you are in expedited removal proceedings, you have the right to request a credible-fear interview — a screening to determine whether you have a credible fear of persecution or torture if returned to your country.2
  • Withholding of removal & CAT protection. Even if you missed the one-year deadline or cannot pursue a regular asylum claim, you may be eligible for withholding of removal or protection under the Convention Against Torture. These protections remain available during the pause.3
  • Bond hearing rights. If you are detained, you have the right to a bond hearing to argue for release. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at this hearing — however, the government will not appoint one for you.4
  • Do not agree to voluntary departure without legal advice. If immigration officials offer you “voluntary departure,” do not agree without consulting an attorney. Voluntary departure has serious consequences for future immigration relief.5

Iran-specific considerations

Common asylum grounds for Iranians include: political opinion, religion or conversion from Islam, ethnicity, gender-based persecution, and sexual orientation or gender identity.

IRGC & military service

If you served in the Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) or certain other military units, your asylum claim may face statutory bars under INA § 208(b)(2), 8 U.S.C. § 1158(b)(2). The two most relevant bars are:

  • The persecutor bar, which prohibits asylum for anyone who ordered, incited, assisted, or otherwise participated in the persecution of others on a protected ground.
  • The terrorist organization bar, which can apply because the IRGC has been designated by the U.S. government as a Foreign Terrorist Organization.

Even mandatory or conscripted service can raise these issues, and an experienced asylum attorney is essential. Discuss your military background honestly and in detail with your attorney.6

Document procurement

Many Iranians cannot obtain documents from the Iranian government. Immigration courts apply a “whole person” assessment and may accept alternative evidence when documents are genuinely impossible to obtain. The burden is on you to explain why specific documents are unavailable. An attorney can help you present this evidence.7

Consular notification risks

Avoid contacting the Iranian consulate during an asylum claim

Be very cautious about contacting the Iranian consulate or embassy in the context of an asylum claim. Doing so could alert the Iranian government to your claim. Consult an attorney before contacting any government agency.
